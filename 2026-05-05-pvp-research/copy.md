## Tuesday: Stop Researching in Serial

**Graphic:** `01_parallel_research.png`
**Pillar:** Data & Enrichment

Most GTM research takes 3-4 hours because people do it sequentially.

Crawl the website. Wait.
Find competitors. Wait.
Read reviews. Wait.
Identify signals. Wait.

We run 4 tracks simultaneously:
- Company Intel (positioning, pricing, features)
- Competitive Intel (5-7 competitors, gaps)
- Customer Intel (verbatim quotes from G2, Capterra)
- Signal Intel (triggers, timing, data sources)

Same depth. 8 minutes instead of 4 hours.

The trick is each track is independent. Nothing in Track 3 depends on Track 2. So why wait?

This morning we ran research on a UK MRP software company. 47 sources. 25 customer quotes. 7 competitors analyzed. 7 deployment-ready PVPs.

Parallelism isn't a hack. It's just not doing the slow thing when you don't have to.

What's slowing down your research process?

## Wednesday: The ISO 9001 Signal

**Graphic:** `02_iso_signal.png`
**Pillar:** GTM Infrastructure

Some buying signals are obvious. Funding round. New hire.

The best signals have three qualities:
1. Hard deadline (creates real urgency)
2. Detectable (you can find them externally)
3. Provable (you have evidence you solve it)

ISO 9001 certification pursuit is one of those signals.

Companies pursuing certification have an audit date. Can't extend it. Can't fake traceability for the auditor. Spreadsheets fail the audit.

And you can find them:
- Quality Manager job postings
- LinkedIn posts mentioning "ISO journey"
- Certification body announcements
- Industry association directories

One customer we researched said Statii was "instrumental in helping us secure ISO 9001 certification."

That's not positioning. That's proof.

When you're building your outbound list, look for signals with deadlines. The urgency is real. The timing is detectable. And the outcome is measurable.

What signals are you tracking that have hard deadlines?

## Thursday: Your Secret Weapon - Founder Credibility

**Graphic:** `03_founder_credibility.png`
**Pillar:** Founder-Led Sales

Generic positioning: "We're a cloud-based MRP for SME manufacturers."

Every competitor can say that. And they do.

Here's what we found researching a UK software company yesterday:

The founders - Mark Kemp and Mark Thornhill - owned a metal fabrication business for 30+ years before building software. They built the product to solve their own problems. The software started as an internal tool.

That's not a marketing story. That's an irreplicable moat.

MRPeasy can't claim 30 years in metal fab. Neither can Katana or Odoo. It's authentic because it's true.

The PVP writes itself: "MRP built by fabricators, for fabricators."

When you're doing customer research, dig for the founder story. How did this company actually start? What problem did the founders personally experience?

That credibility transfers to every prospect conversation. And competitors can't copy it.

What's the founder story you're not telling?
