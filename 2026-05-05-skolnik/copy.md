## Tuesday: The Certification Moat

**Graphic:** `01_certification_moat.png`
**Pillar:** GTM Infrastructure

Everyone says "we're ISO certified" like it means something.

It doesn't. Every competitor has ISO 9001. It's table stakes.

Here's what actually creates a moat:

I just researched a Chicago drum manufacturer. They hold NQA-1 Nuclear Quality Assurance certification. Most competitors have generic ISO 9001.

Result? They own the mid-market nuclear segment. Competitors can't even bid on those deals.

The certification pyramid works like this:
- Self-reported quality claims (meaningless)
- ISO 9001 / general standards (table stakes)
- Industry-specific certs (differentiator)
- Niche regulatory certs (moat)

The harder the cert is to get, the fewer competitors can follow you there.

What's the hardest credential in your space that most competitors don't have?

## Wednesday: The Ghosting Problem

**Graphic:** `02_ghosting_problem.png`
**Pillar:** Founder-Led Sales

Mid-market companies get ghosted by enterprise vendors.

I keep hearing the same story:

"We sent an RFQ, waited 3 weeks, followed up twice, maybe got a quote. Maybe."

The enterprise vendors are optimized for $500K orders. Your $25K order goes to the bottom of the pile.

Real example from a manufacturer I researched:

Major supplier: 8-12 weeks for a quote
Specialized vendor: 48-hour turnaround

Same product. Same specs. Wildly different experience.

The specialized vendor wins because they treat $25K customers like $250K customers.

This is the wedge smaller companies should exploit:

You can't beat Goliath on scale.
You can beat them on speed.
You can beat them on responsiveness.
You can beat them on giving a damn.

When did you last have to chase a vendor more than twice for a quote?

## Thursday: Nuclear-Grade Positioning

**Graphic:** `03_nuclear_grade.png`
**Pillar:** RevOps for Early-Stage

"Engineered to nuclear standards. Available for commercial applications."

That's actual positioning from a $25M manufacturer I researched.

Here's why it's brilliant:

They serve nuclear power plants. That requires NQA-1 certification - the hardest quality standard in industrial manufacturing.

But here's the play:

They also sell to pharma and chemical companies who don't need nuclear certification. These customers pay premium prices anyway.

Why? The halo effect.

If you're good enough for nuclear, you're good enough for anything. The credential creates implicit trust that generic ISO 9001 can't match.

The framework:
1. Get the hardest cert in your space
2. Lead with it in positioning
3. Sell downstream to segments that value credibility

The hardest certification you hold becomes your easiest sale everywhere else.

What's the "nuclear-grade" positioning opportunity in your market?
